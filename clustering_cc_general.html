Abstract¶

This study explores customer segmentation using two clustering algorithms, K‑Means and DBSCAN, applied to the Credit Card Customer Segmentation dataset. Six financial features: BALANCE, PURCHASES, CASH_ADVANCE, CREDIT_LIMIT, PAYMENTS, and MINIMUM_PAYMENTS were analyzed after cleaning and scaling. K‑Means provided structured clusters based on centroid optimization, while DBSCAN revealed density‑based groupings and isolated outliers. Validation metrics and cluster profiling were used to evaluate performance. The comparison highlights the strengths and limitations of each algorithm, offering insights into which method is more practical for marketing teams seeking to understand customer behavior.¶

Objectives¶

1. To prepare and clean the dataset, ensuring reliable inputs for clustering analysis.¶
2. To apply K‑Means and DBSCAN clustering, evaluate their performance using silhouette, Davies‑Bouldin, and Calinski‑Harabasz metrics, and profile customer segments.¶
3. To compare both algorithms side‑by‑side and recommend the most suitable approach for customer segmentation in a business context.¶

Summary¶

Results showed that DBSCAN achieved a higher Silhoutte score and a Davies-Bouldin index closer to zero (closer to zero is better for Davies-Bouldin index) while K-means had a higher Calinksi-Harabasz index. DBSCAN had better cluster cohesion while K-means (with K=3) had better cluster separation and internal density.¶

Choosing K=3 strikes the best balance between the two evaluation methods: the Elbow Method shows a clear inflection around three clusters, indicating that adding more clusters beyond this point yields diminishing returns in reducing inertia, while the Silhouette Analysis, though highest at K=2, still maintains a reasonably strong score at K=3 compared to larger values. This means that three clusters capture more structure in the data than just two, without fragmenting it into poorly separated groups as seen with higher K values. In practice, K=3 provides a compromise. Clusters that are compact and distinct enough to be meaningful, while also reflecting more nuanced patterns in the dataset than a binary split.

from sklearn.cluster import DBSCAN

eps_values = [2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14, 16]
results = []

for eps in eps_values:
    dbscan = DBSCAN(eps=eps, min_samples=5)
    labels_test = dbscan.fit_predict(X_scaled)

    n_clusters = len(set(labels_test)) - (1 if -1 in labels_test else 0)
    n_noise = list(labels_test).count(-1)
    noise_pct = n_noise / len(labels_test) * 100

    results.append([eps, n_clusters, n_noise, f"{noise_pct:.2f}%"])

df_results = pd.DataFrame(results, columns=["eps", "Clusters Found", "Noise Points", "Noise %"])

Interpretation:

  • At eps = 2, DBSCAN identified three clusters and flagged 91 noise points (~1.05%). This is the only configuration that produced multiple clusters, and the small noise percentage is desirable because it shows DBSCAN successfully isolated a few outliers without discarding too many customers.
  • At eps = 4, 6, and 8, DBSCAN collapsed into a single cluster, with only a handful of points marked as noise. As eps increases, DBSCAN becomes more permissive, merging all customers into one group and reducing noise, but at the cost of losing meaningful segmentation.
  • At eps ≥ 10, DBSCAN produced one cluster with zero noise. At these values, the algorithm treats the dataset as one dense blob, which defeats the purpose of DBSCAN, where its strength lies in finding density-based structure and isolating outliers.

Conclusion: At least four eps values were tested, with additional values checked for completeness. The most informative configuration was eps = 2, min_samples = 5, because it produced multiple clusters and a small but nontrivial set of noise points. Larger values of eps failed to capture the density variations in the dataset, making DBSCAN ineffective for segmentation.

Recommendation: K-Means Clustering¶

While Density Based Clustering (DBSCAN) achieved a higher Silhouette Score (0.706 versus 0.513 for K-means clustering) and a Davies-Bouldin index closer to zero (0.501 versus 1.283 for K-means clustering), K-means has a higher Calinski-Harabasz Index (2515.28 compared to 83.80 for DBSCAN). DBSCAN, with its higher Silhouette score and Davies-Bouldin index, implies better cohesion, where the points are inside its assigned cluster, far from other clusters. While K-means clustering with its higher Calinski-Harabasz index implies a better separation of the clusters. Given the non-spherical shape caused by the right skewness of the data, DBSCAN would be a better fit, structurally.

From the results, most of the noise from Density Based Clustering (DBSCAN) was assigned to Cluster 2 of K-means, the cluster that includes customers with very high purchases and credit limits. This makes sense since these customers are more likely to be outliers, as seen in the histograms of the six features all being right skewed, including Balance and Purchases. In a business context, if the outliers are customers with high purchases and credit limits, and therefore a higher purchasing power, then they are to be generalized as part of Cluster 2 or 1 instead of being classified as noise. Revenue can be simplified as $price \times quantity$, which means that the marketing department can either generate sales by selling a large quantity that appeals to the majority (customers part of Cluster 0; low balance and moderate spenders) or by selling at a higher yet still profitable price towards customers part of Cluster 2.

However, as defined previously, Cluster 2 (and most of the values marked as noise for DBSCAN) contain customers that are already high-spending customers with high payments, credit limits and payments, which allows them to spend more, which is the goal of a marketing team: to target customers that can spend and use their credit card. It is worth noting that there were still around thirty customers that were assigned to Cluster 1 of K-means from the DBSCAN noise; these customers have high balances and credit limits but with moderate cash advances. These customers cannot be discarded as noise, as done by DBSCAN, because they can still be generalized as customers with high purchasing power. Assigning the outliers to clusters 1 and 2, as done by K-means, allows a marketing team to focus on high spending customers, even customers with the potential to spend due to a high purchasing power, thus giving a marketing department more flexibility in their strategies. Therefore, K-means clustering lands a recommendation due to the given context of a business and marketing.
